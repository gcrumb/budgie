Revenue, Spending and the Deficit

The 2014 budget leaves a surplus - revenues are expected to exceed spending - by almost VUV 600 million. This follows a series of sizeable deficits from 2007 to 2012. Treasury officials tend to strive for a balanced budget on the basis that fiscal deficits are likely to cause cash flow problems.

Domestic and Foreign Debt

The Vanuatu government has come close to exhausting its ability to take on domestic debt. The standard sources of commercial banks and Vanuatu National Provident Fund appear uninterested in taking on more government, in particular those debts of a shorter period. This is a challenge to the cash-flow management of the government.

Reflecting this tight environment, the government asked the Reserve Bank to purchase VUV 100 million in bonds at the end of 2013.

Many potential new lending agreements from external concessional lending, in particular Chinese EXIM bank, are absent from this data. This is likely to shift Vanuatu’s debt burden significantly.

Economic Activity

Vanuatu’s economic activity has been slowing over recent years reflecting a weakening donor-driven construction activity, a weakening in the property market and low commodity prices. The Vanuatu government expects all of these things to change over the coming years, driving a strong recovery. Domestic inflationary pressures may be on the horizon.

2013 Budget Performance

The government overspent in a number of areas in 2013. Most notable is the overspending on government scholarships, which has created a significant change in the Vanuatu government’s education budget over the last few years- this is discussed in more detail in this case study.
