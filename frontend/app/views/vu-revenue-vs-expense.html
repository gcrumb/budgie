Economic Activity

The government’s assessment of the state of the economy provides the backdrop for the creation of their budget. It not only gives officials an idea of how government revenues are likely to perform but also is used to shape the governments decisions about how much to tax, spend and borrow. For the rest of us, it gives an indication of which way economic opportunities may be moving.

Growth by Sector

(Percentage change)

In the lead up to 2008 Vanuatu’s economy experienced unprecedented economic growth following a period of reform and government stability. Donor investment, tourism growth, a booming property sector and the liberalization of the telecoms industry drove Vanuatu’s economy at historically high growth rates.

With the global financial crisis came the collapse in property investment, low commodity prices and, for a while, slower than projected growth in tourism numbers. As a result, banks were left with increasing numbers of non-performing loans. Below we see the impact of slowing donor activity and property investment on the construction sector. The agricultural sector, providing the incomes for the majority of the population, has suffered from swings in commodity prices and the lows experienced from 2011.

The government’s outlook remains optimistic with the prospect of a large up-scaling in donor activity around the corner and a recovery in commodity prices- such as copra. Knowing precisely when this donor activity will kick off remains a challenge for the government. However, when this activity occurs, inflation could rise rapidly.

2013 Budget Performance

Unplanned spending shows the areas of public spending that come under pressure during the course of the year, either because of a lack of planning or because of political pressures to spend outside of the budget. These can sometimes undermine government’s own stated priorities.

Unplanned Spending in 2013

The government has a number of unplanned areas of spending in 2013, which totaled to VUV 400 million. Most notable is the sizable overspending on government scholarships for the second year running. This has created a significant change in the Vanuatu government’s education budget over the last few years- this is discussed in more detail in this case study.

Revenue, Spending and the Deficit

These tend to be the headline figures to come out of any government budget. Importantly, it gives an indication of whether the government is spending more than it receives in revenues or vice-versa. If the government spends more than it receives it has a budget deficit and must fill this hole through borrowing.

Expenses vs Revenues

(Millions of Vatu)

The 2014 budget leaves a surplus - revenues are expected to exceed spending - by almost VUV 600 million, following a series of sizeable deficits from 2007 to 2012.

Given the small size of the revenue base and the limited domestic borrowing opportunities – including of short maturities - the government works towards balancing the government budget. However, the nature of the political and capacity challenges make shaping an active fiscal policy – running a surplus in good times and deficit in bad - is more difficult for Vanuatu than for other countries.

Revenue growth remains a long-term challenge of the Vanuatu government if it wishes to maintain and build on the quality of public services. New initiatives were discussed in a revenue forum at the beginning of 2013.

Vanuatu’s Debt

To finance budget deficits the government takes on debt. Taking on some form of debt is necessary for the smooth running of government operations. However, if it’s not carefully managed, debt can have important implications by increasing vulnerability to economic shocks, while repayments can eat quickly into the funding of other government services.

Vanuatu’s Repayments

Debt repayments are those government revenues being used to pay back previous debts and their interest. For some Pacific islands governments with limited revenue sources, these payments compete with other spending priorities.

Debt Repayments

(Millions of Vatu)

By the government forecasts, from 2011 to 2014 interest repayments have increased by half and foreign debt repayment has almost doubled. Over the same time revenues, increased by less than a fifth, meaning that these repayments are eating into government budgets. For the Vanuatu government, at present their investments are not providing a direct financial return to prevent this.

Donor Spending

Donor spending makes up a significant proportion of spending in some areas of Vanuatu government spending. Government and donor priorities should be carefully aligned.

Donor & Govt. Spending

(Millions of Vatu)

The government budget books list around VUV 7 billion worth of donor spending in kind and in cash. This is presented alongside the government’s own contribution to the same areas.
