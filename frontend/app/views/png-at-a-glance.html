GDP - The LNG Story

The government’s assessment of the state of the economy provides the backdrop for the creation of their budget. It not only gives officials an idea of how government revenues are likely to perform but also is used to shape the governments decisions about how much to tax, spend and borrow. For the rest of us, it gives an indication of which way economic opportunities may be moving.

The prospects for economic growth over coming years may look impressive at first glance, however it is clear that the lion’s share of the economic activity will be driven directly by the start of LNG production. While economic activity in agriculture, forestry and fisheries is likely to perform better in 2014, activity outside the mining sector remains particularly slow. In 2014 non-mining GDP is expected to grow by just 1.6 percent.

LNG production will drive an initial 70 percent increase in mineral exports, moving the PNG economy towards becoming a net exporter of goods and services. Yet over the next five years from 2013, agricultural, forestry, fishing exports are expected to expand by a modest 10 percent. Here lays a central challenge facing the PNG government: can the revenues from LNG exports be carefully managed to share the benefits of this economic growth? Partly this requires that increases in government spending must be converted into meaningful improvements in public services.

Spending vs Revenue- More today, less tomorrow

These tend to be the headline figures to come out of any government budget. Importantly, it gives an indication of whether the government is spending more than it receives in revenues or vice-versa. If the government spends more than it receives it has a budget deficit and must fill this hole through borrowing.

The 2014 PNG budget seems to push further the philosophy presented in the previous budgets of spending more today for less tomorrow. This can be seen not just in the faster than planned growth in spending of the last two years, but also in the projections for the next five years which require a rapid tightening in spending. These plans appear, at first glance, somewhat optimistic.

Growth in revenue and spending plans for the PNG government paint an interesting picture. In 2013 spending was increased by over 20 percent to invest in ‘key enablers’ of development (including health, education, infrastructure, law and order), opening up a sizeable budget deficit. This deficit was justified as a measure to help stimulate economic activity prior to the start of LNG production.

In 2014 the government offered another significant jump in spending. However, this year revenue is expected to buck the overall trend in the economy and perform well due to increased compliance and efficiency savings. Such drivers of revenue growth can sometimes (but not always) indicate that the budget is coming under pressure - a sign that political pressures may be on increased spending, leaving officials to fill the funding hole as best they can to appear fiscally responsibly. Revenues in 2014 will therefore have to be carefully managed to ensure they do not undershoot expectations, creating a larger hole in the budget and forcing further borrowing.

In 2015, the PNG government is faced with a significant challenge of hitting hard on the spending brakes to limit the size of the budget deficit. Given PNG’s debt situation (see below) and the upcoming economic activity, this strategy of aligning the budget with its medium term targets is appropriate. We know however, from the experience of PNG and other countries in the region, that such plans are likely to come under significant political pressure. This is particularly likely when we see that 2015 is expected to have just 2 percent increase in spending (less than inflation) at a time while revenue growth is above 9 percent. This will become clearer when the 2015 budget is presented at the end of the year, but in any case, it provides another interesting test of practicalities of managing an active fiscal policy.

The Debt Tightrope

To finance budget deficits the government takes on debt. Taking on some form of debt is necessary for the smooth running of government operations. However, if it’s not carefully managed, debt can have important implications by increasing vulnerability to economic shocks, while repayments can eat quickly into the funding of other government services. Debt- to-GDP (total debt as a proportion of what the country produces) tends to be an important indicator.

At the same time as the 2014 Budget was tabled, legislation was passed for the amendment of the Fiscal Responsibility Act to give further room for borrowing though moving the Debt/GDP limits from 30 to 35 percent. According to the government’s own projections debt indicators should just about remain within these new bounds, however this relies on robust revenue receipts and economic growth projections. If either of these projections is not met, debt as a proportion of GDP will be significantly higher and likely move the government even further from their original limits of fiscal responsibility.

The story of government debt reflects the spending and revenue raising decisions discussed above. In 2014 external concessional loans saw a marked increase but is forecast to fall rapidly. At first glance it seems fairly unlikely that these projections will be met given the pressures to take on concessional loans in the region.

Government Planning and Assessment

Underpinning the government budget is an array of plans and assessments. Ideally such documents should inform many of the decisions that are taken in the government budget.

  • Papua New Guinea Fiscal Responsibility Act: This provides the limits for fiscal responsibility for the PNG government.
  • Budget Strategy Paper (2014): Planning for the 2014 budget
  • Mid Year Economic and Fiscal Outlook (MYEFO) 2013: Progress on the 2013 Budget.

Planning for the Future

  • Medium Term Fiscal Strategy (2013-2017): Longer term budget planning
  • Medium Term Development Plan (2011-2015): Directing spending priorities
  • PNG Development Strategic Plan (2010-2030): Overarching development plan
  • Vision 2050: Overarching development plan
