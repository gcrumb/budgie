Opening the government budgets of Pacific island states

In the Pacific region our governments face many challenges. Our dispersed islands can make public service delivery particularly costly and difficult. At the same time the size of our countries and economies can often limit the resources at our disposal and hinder the capacity of our civil services to deliver government policy. The government machine is often thinly spread, both geographically and across many policy priorities.

It for this reason that difficulties facing public services tend to be the complicated combination of a lack of resources, poor management of public finances and the need to develop strong, locally-driven, policy solutions. This is a difficult story to untangle for those small number of officials trying to make sense of these issues behind the doors of government Ministries.

The government budget is the foundation of much of these discussions and gives us a place to begin our considerations delivery of public services. Unfortunately, only a few countries manage to make budget information accessible, even fewer find a way of communicating the contents well and almost no one is working to actively engage those outside the civil service. However, from my own personal experience, I know that Ministries of Finance are very stretched- issues of communication and engagement are understandably towards the end of a Treasury official’s to-do list.

It is for this reason that I am pleased to support of the Pacific Institute of Public Policy (PiPP), who, unique to the region, are not just calling for more accountability but have started the process of working directly alongside governments in the region to open up these discussions to a broader audience.

To some extent this work will help support government accountability - after all the government is spending public funds (dare I say it, ‘your money’). However, what PiPP has rightly identified is that engaging people in the budgeting process is also about stimulating informed public policy debate: where should government spending be directed? What are the best ways to raise revenue? How do we improve management of public finances? How should we deliver healthcare or education?

These are often discussion driven by a few and all too often by those outside the region.

The launch of PiPP’s engaging and interactive approach to discussing issues in government budgets is the first step towards an end to the closed-door discussions of public policy. Only through bringing these statistics and policy discussions together and active engagement with citizens, parliamentarians, NGOs and the press, can we begin to offer a richer environment to shape good policy, improved management of finances and stronger delivery of public services.

Odo Tevi
Former Governor, Reserve Bank of Vanuatu and PiPP Board Member
