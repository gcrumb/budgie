Open Budgets: The Case of Government Scholarships in Vanuatu

For Vanuatu and the Solomon Islands, the issue of government-sponsored scholarships, an aspirational policy to expand opportunities for youth, has become increasingly entangled in their more surreptitious role as a tool of patronage for Ministers and MPs. Over recent years scholarships have become part of the political toolkit to secure coalitions and maintain government numbers: stick with us and we can provide your children with scholarships.

This use of scholarships has unintentionally transformed the face of the government budget, creating a rapidly increasing area of overspend that Treasury officials struggle to contain. In the space of three years, Vanuatu has seen its scholarship spending more than triple without any explicit change in policy or budget. Spending on scholarships in 2014 is now expected to be VUV 500 million more than what was budgeted in 2012 and can be seen as one of the most notable changes in spending priorities since the Vanuatu Government’s Comprehensive Reform Programme.

This overspending is yet another challenge for the overburdened managers of Vanuatu’s public finances. Yet it's difficult to challenge this kind of mismanagement of funds. It is too easy to sidestep the issue claiming it as an ambitious realignment of government policy to focus on higher education- the money, after all, is being used for our children. It is perhaps for this reason that, government scholarships in Vanuatu, despite being seen as an example of corruption, do not muster the same level of anger as siphoning of funds, for example, to the supporters of political parties.

If we measure a policy by ambition and ambition by money, tripling scholarship spending does represent a success of a sort, even if it was the product of complicated, closed doors, political processes. However, government policies should be measured by the their outcomes, the costs of achieving these (both in term of money and how else we could have used these funds) and whether the resources are fairly divvied up. By these standards, does this level of spending, by chance, reflect a solid policy to invest in youth as the leaders and workforce of the future?

Understanding the trade-offs

The costs of scholarships must be set against the question that inspires much of the economics profession, as well as those who want to hold their government to account: is there a better way to spend our public money?

One first consideration is how much does the programme cost. What are we sacrificing to place this additional VUV 500 million into scholarships? At VUV 778 million (318 million more than budgeted in 2014) this programme now costs half the Ministry of Health government budget and actually is more costly than running eight of Vanuatu’s thirteen ministries.

From the perspective of the education this means we have seen an implicit shifting of priorities of successive governments from primary and secondary to tertiary education. The latter now consumes over 20% of the Ministry of Education budget, compared with less than 5% in 2008 (see here to explore the education budget). This raises important questions of fairness even before we even consider how well this money is used.

This issue is further underscored when we consider how well the scholarship programme functions as part of broader education policy. After all, if scholarships are administered fairly and offer significant benefits, an additional VUV 500 million to the scholarship kitty may not be an unreasonable decision. Unfortunately, there is reason to believe that additional funds for scholarships are not a good investment for Vanuatu or those students who take them on. This implies that this sizeable sum may have found greater benefits elsewhere.

The result of Ministerial discretion over the administration of scholarships is that too many students are accepted that do not reach the required academic standards. This is not just a matter of fairness it also has broader implications in that many fail to complete their degrees or are forced to repeat years- this is costly and helps no one; certainly not the youth that were perhaps nudged along the wrong learning path.

For those who are successful, job opportunities remain stubbornly hard to come across, as scholarship policy is not targeted towards skills gaps. Businesses continue to complain that students aim for easier courses that tend to be lack relevance for the work force. So perhaps most worrying is that the statistics from other areas of education remain poor. If you start school, you currently have about a 1 in 10 chance that you will make it through to the end of secondary - this statistic does not appear to be getting better quickly.

The problems facing primary and secondary education are not just related to money. Amongst other things it is a problem of poor accountability of funds, rundown infrastructure and pockets of poor quality of teaching- these can be found discussed Vanuatu Education Sector Public Expenditure Review, published at the end of 2012. However, what this report also highlights is that the government’s goals of universal primary education (UPE) and basic education (UBE), are difficult to meet on the basis they would require an extra VUV 510 million in the Department of Education’s recurrent budget from 2013-2017 &emdash; ironically, the same amount that has subsequently been added to the scholarships budget. It is these kind of decisions that mean the government’s own goals for primary education will continue to be encumbered by a lack of places for the official primary school age group.

What this means is that the current education system still struggles to offer Ni-Vanuatu the skills to find work and compete against an increasingly international workforce not just from Australia but also from as far afield as Mauritius, the Philippines and China. Most indicators suggest that this not moving so quickly. From the opening up of Vanuatu government budget, aside from the budget constraints facing lower levels of schooling, we also now know that spending from both the donors and government on technical and vocational training (TVET), the area designed to provide practical skills for workforce, is only a quarter of that spent on technical and higher education. If we wish to see tangible benefits in this area, investments in education are worthwhile- however expanding government scholarships at this rate is unlikely to be the place to start.

The rapid growth in scholarship overspending for the last few years cannot be ignored for much longer &emdash; diversion of funds to this area is diversion of funds from elsewhere... and for little benefit. Perhaps for good reason, such additional spending on scholarships does not form part of the government’s Priority Action Agenda that calls for “a Just, an Educated, Healthy and Wealthy Vanuatu”. However, if we wish to realign spending with these government priorities, the mechanics that underpin the overspending on scholarships are easy to fix. After all, this requires little more than limiting scholarship numbers, enforcing strict educational standards and removing ministerial discretion. Yet as with a great deal of areas, finding the political will is less easy.

Few tend to bemoan giving extra money to the young to pursue further education, even if the spending is politically driven, unplanned and administered unfairly. At some point this has to change. Down the line, we do not wish to be asking how much we have sacrificed to fund an unfair scheme that struggles to serve its purpose.
