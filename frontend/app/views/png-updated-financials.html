Open Budgets: The Gap Widens in Papua New Guinea

Much has been made over the last couple of days over an analysis we published on Papua New Guinea's updated financial outlook. What's really in it?

The governmentâ€™s assessment of the state of the economy provides the backdrop for the creation of their budget. It not only gives officials an idea of how government revenues are likely to perform but also is used to shape the governments decisions about how much to tax, spend and borrow. The accuracy of its predictions therefore have a direct impact on a country's fate. The numbers below belong to the Treasury department of the government of Papua New Guinea.

The first chart we see above shows a deficit that ballooned in 2013 in anticipation of increased natural resource revenues. To some extent, this is to be expected; PNG was effectively creating an entirely new industrial sector at that time, so some increased spending is par for the course.

The narrative as expressed in the 2014 budget was that spending would decrease slightly, and revenues would remain high. This would have the effect of the deficit being gently reduced over time as revenues increase.

Fast forward six months, and we find that spending has not in fact slowed down, and spending remains more or less consistent with what was originally planned. The result is a potential increase in an already large deficit. But if we read the fine print of the Treasury's Mid Year Fiscal and Economic Update, we find the quotation above, hinting that all might not be as rosy as predicted.
